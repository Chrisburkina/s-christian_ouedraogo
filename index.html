Public debt in african countries

Between intergenerational justice and injustice

📅 Jannuary, 17th 2026 | 🕘 17:48

The question of public debt is widely debated in African countries. Today, more and more voices are rising in public opinion to condemn it. However, some testify in this trial of public debt without real knowledge of the subject. Therefore, I'm interested in the question in this blog, with a scientific approach but in a literal way, in order to contribute to the debate, and potentially give my perspective on things. Indeed, the debt is paradoxically much more significant than one might think. To begin with, common wisdom regarding public debt is the interpretation according to which it is positive to the extent that it allows the State to invest, which creates income for households, which then begin to consume, which creates markets for businesses, which in turn invest. Growth is stimulated by ricochet. This is what neoclassical economics teaches us, notably through the Keynesian multiplier.The idea of a negative effect originates withsome extensions of Ricardo, for whom economic agents are capable of saving instead of consuming, in anticipation of future tax increases, which are intended to repay the debt. This literally breaks the Keynesian cycle, and debates exist on this topic. Even if the majority of African populations meet the condition of monetary illusion, a relatively negative effect of debt is still observed in Africa, and the debate is ongoing. Yet, well-understood debt remains a lever for growth. It depends on how the funds are invested. It is not uncommon to hear that debt forces future generations to pay amounts they did not incur. But if the debt is well invested, it only becomes, in this sense, a tool of intergenerational justice. Indeed, these generations will come to benefit from better infrastructures, better systems, without having had to contribute to them. A debt that is used to build a road increases in advance the margins of future generations, by reducing transportation costs. The lack of a certain facility can complicate matters for the current generation, forcing some to give up. This facility, created through debt, will make things easier for the next generation. It is normal that they pay the price. That, in my opinion, is a major advantage of well-used debt. It allows us to overcome time. If an infrastructure cannot be built and profitable within one generation, what incentive does the current generation have to consider it? Should the future generation come a generation earlier to start working? Debt allows us to invite the future into the present, giving it a temporal advantage. This raises the question: How do we know what future generations will need? Once again, we delve into the debate about the effective use of debt. First and foremost, debt must be fully invested. If a portion is misappropriated, it becomes a serious problem for the future. If a debt investment fails to produce results, the future pays for the mistakes of the present. Therefore, it must be invested in key sectors that drive the economy, using sound research and methodology. Other investment methods exist, but those financed by debt must be resolutely future-oriented, not focused on immediate needs, however noble those needs may be. Making life easier today must be done with today's funds, not future ones. The debt must also be constantly adjusted in line with tax revenues to ensure complete intergenerational equity. It is also essential to avoid borrowing in foreign currency. Even if one euro is worth 655 CFA francs, it is preferable for a country in French West Africa (AOF) to owe 655 CFA francs rather than one euro, as there is no guarantee of exchange rate stability. The final point to monitor concerns the debt burden. It is crucial to recognize the importance of containing it to prevent it from discouraging private investment.

Master thésis project defense

Research related to the porter hypothésis

📅 December, 16th 2025 | 🕘 15:30 | 📍 Dijon

Today I defended my research project with a view to writing my master's 1 thesis in Innovations and Public Policies. This project presents the subject on which I want to work, a review of the existing literature about the question, my theoretical contribution, as well as the methodology to use. This project was validated by the jury and will therefore be treated until the end of this academic year. It will offer a new look at Porter's hypothesis, particularly on the ways through wich environmental regulations lead shareholders to modify the contractual structure with managers, leading to more innovation and potentially to the validating of Porter's hypothesis.
